================================================================================
SUPPORTING INFORMATION / EXPERIMENTAL SECTION: STRESS RELAXATION ANALYSIS METHODOLOGY
================================================================================

This document provides a formal, scientifically rigorous description of the data 
preprocessing, viscoelastic modeling, temperature-dependent kinetics, continuous 
relaxation spectra, and master curve construction protocols used in this study. 
This text may be directly adapted for the Experimental Section or Supporting 
Information (SI) of research manuscripts.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. Data Preprocessing, Smoothing, and Normalization
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Raw stress relaxation data (relaxation modulus G(t) or E(t) as a function of 
time t) were preprocessed to remove experimental artifacts, initial loading 
transients, and high-frequency noise. The signal-to-noise ratio was enhanced by 
applying a Savitzky-Golay smoothing filter (second-order polynomial, with a 
dynamic window size optimized to 10% of the total dataset length). 

To isolate the true onset of viscoelastic relaxation and eliminate initial strain-
loading artifacts (e.g., motor ramp-up or inertial overshoot), the start of the 
relaxation process (t = 0) was defined by identifying the maximum modulus (peak 
force) in the smoothed curve. Any initial data points preceding this peak were 
discarded. The time axis was then shifted to establish the origin:
    t_shifted = t - t_peak + t_offset
where a regularizing time offset (t_offset = 0.01 s) was added to ensure 
numerical stability and prevent logarithmic divergence during subsequent power-law 
fitting. 

The relaxation modulus was normalized to the initial modulus (G_0 or E_0), defined 
as the maximum value within the first 10% of the trimmed curve to suppress 
outlier influence, yielding the normalized modulus:
    g(t) = G(t) / G_0   or   e(t) = E(t) / E_0

Data points exhibiting thermal drift or instrument-related upward forces at long 
times (detected as a systematic modulus rise exceeding 10% from a local minimum 
near the tail) were automatically truncated to preserve fitting integrity.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. Viscoelastic Stress Relaxation Modeling
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The normalized stress relaxation curves, g(t), were fitted to three physics-based 
rheological models using a non-linear least-squares fitting algorithm based on 
the Levenberg-Marquardt method:

A. Maxwell Model (Single Exponential Decay)
   This model represents the relaxation of a simple viscoelastic liquid with a 
   single, discrete relaxation time scale:
       g(t) = exp( -t / \tau )
   where \tau is the characteristic relaxation time.

B. Kohlrausch-Williams-Watts (KWW) Model (Stretched Exponential)
   This model accounts for a continuous distribution of relaxation times, typical 
   of polydisperse polymer networks and glassy dynamics:
       g(t) = exp( -(t / \tau)^\beta )
   where \tau is the characteristic relaxation time and \beta is the stretching 
   exponent (0 < \beta <= 1). A lower value of \beta indicates a broader 
   distribution of relaxation processes, whereas \beta = 1 recovers the single 
   Maxwellian decay.

C. Dual-mode KWW Model (Double Stretched Exponential)
   This model captures multi-scale dynamics where two distinct relaxation 
   mechanisms occur simultaneously (e.g., rapid glassy or matrix relaxation 
   coupled with slower dynamic bond-exchange flow in vitrimers):
       g(t) = f * exp( -(t / \tau_1)^{\beta_1} ) + (1 - f) * exp( -(t / \tau_2)^{\beta_2} )
   where f represents the fraction of the fast mode, \tau_1 and \beta_1 are the 
   relaxation time and stretching exponent of the fast process, and \tau_2 and 
   \beta_2 are those of the slow process.

To prevent overfitting and ensure physical validity, model selection was automated 
using the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC). The AIC evaluates the quality of 
each fit by balancing the goodness of fit (residual sum of squares, RSS) against 
model complexity (number of adjustable parameters, k):
    AIC = 2*k + n * ln(RSS / n)
where n is the number of data points. The model yielding the minimum AIC was 
selected as the mathematically and physically optimal representation of the 
material's relaxation behavior.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. Temperature-Dependent Kinetics and Activation Energy (E_a)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The temperature dependence of the characteristic relaxation times (\tau) was 
analyzed using the classical Arrhenius equation:
    ln(\tau) = ln(\tau_0) + E_a / (R * T)
where \tau_0 is the pre-exponential factor (s), E_a is the flow activation energy 
for network topology rearrangement (kJ/mol), R is the universal gas constant 
(8.314462 J/(mol*K)), and T is the absolute temperature (K).

Linear regression of ln(\tau) against 1000/T was performed to determine the slope 
(slope = E_a / (1000 * R)) and intercept. To provide statistical rigor, the 
standard deviation of the activation energy (\Delta E_a) was calculated from the 
standard error of the regression slope (stderr_slope) via error propagation:
    \Delta E_a = stderr_slope * R * 1000

For dynamic networks near the glass transition temperature (T_g), deviations from 
Arrhenius behavior were monitored. In such regimes, the Vogel-Fulcher-Tammann 
(VFT) equation was applied:
    ln(\tau) = A + B / (T - T_0)
where A is a pre-exponential factor, B is the steepness parameter (K), and T_0 
is the ideal glass transition temperature (K) at which relaxation time diverges.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. Extrapolation of the Vitrimer Topology Freezing Temperature (T_v)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The vitrimer topology freezing transition temperature (T_v) marks the transition 
from a solid-like network with frozen topology to a liquid-like malleable network 
undergoing active bond exchange. By definition, T_v corresponds to the temperature 
where the material reaches a shear viscosity threshold of \eta = 10^12 Pa*s 
(the conventional glass transition threshold). 

Using the Maxwell relation:
    \tau^* = \eta / G'
the target relaxation time at the threshold (\tau^*) was calculated by dividing 
10^12 Pa*s by the storage modulus (G', entered in MPa). T_v was subsequently 
extrapolated by solving the fitted Arrhenius equation for the target value:
    1000 / T_v(K) = (ln(\tau^*) - intercept) / slope
    T_v(degC) = T_v(K) - 273.15
where slope and intercept are derived from the Arrhenius linear fit of the experimental 
relaxation times.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. Continuous Relaxation Time Spectrum
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The continuous distribution of relaxation times, represented by the relaxation 
spectrum H(\tau), was calculated from the normalized experimental modulus 
g(t) by solving the Fredholm integral equation of the first kind:
    g(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} H(\tau) * exp(-t/\tau) * d(ln \tau)
This ill-posed inverse problem was solved numerically using Tikhonov regularization 
(Ridge regression) over a logarithmically spaced grid of relaxation times (\tau_j) 
spanning beyond the experimental time window:
    Minimize || A * H - g ||^2 + \alpha * || H ||^2
where A_ij = exp(-t_i / \tau_j) is the kernel matrix, \alpha is the regularization 
parameter controlling the smoothness of the spectrum, and the constraint H >= 0 
was strictly enforced to ensure physical validity (non-negative relaxation times).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. Master Curve Construction (Time-Temperature Superposition - TTS)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
To study viscoelastic dynamics over a wider frequency/time domain than is 
experimentally accessible, master curves were constructed via Time-Temperature 
Superposition (TTS). Modulus curves obtained at different temperatures were 
horizontally shifted along the logarithmic time axis relative to a chosen reference 
temperature (T_ref). 

The horizontal shift factor, a_T, was calculated directly from the fitted 
characteristic relaxation times:
    a_T = \tau(T) / \tau(T_ref)
where \tau is extracted from the optimal model fit at each temperature. The shifted 
time scales (t_shifted = t / a_T) from all temperatures were merged and sorted to 
form a continuous master curve, validating the applicability of time-temperature 
equivalence in the dynamic network.
